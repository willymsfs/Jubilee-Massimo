Jubilee of Hope — 2025

The Catholic Jubilee: A Journey into History, Theology, and Hope

Based on a lecture by Dr. Massimo Faggioli (Villanova University)

Introduction to the presentation. We will explore a lecture by Dr. Massimo Faggioli, a distinguished Catholic Church historian. Our goal is to systematically understand the Jubilee's historical origins, its modern evolution, and its profound theological meaning for today, focusing on the 2025 Jubilee of Hope.

Context

Meet the Speaker: Dr. Massimo Faggioli

Professor of Historical Theology at Villanova University.
Author on papacy, Vatican II, global Church.
Columnist (La Croix Int’l, Commonweal).
Introducing the expert whose insights we are studying. Dr. Faggioli is a leading authority on the history of the Catholic Church, the papacy, and the Second Vatican Council.
Definitions

What is a Catholic Jubilee?

  • A holy year of grace, conversion, prayer, and repentance.
  • Distinctly Catholic practice, traditionally a pilgrimage to Rome.
  • Each Jubilee centers on a specific spiritual theme.
Defining the core concept and how it relates—but is not identical—to the biblical jubilee.
Origins

1300: The First Jubilee — Papacy at its Apex

Instituted by Pope Boniface VIII during the era of Christendom: blurred lines between church and state, strong papal authority.

The Jubilee expressed papal power and a unified Latin Christendom.
Cadence

How Often Are Jubilees Held?

  • 1300: 100-year interval
  • Later: 50-year interval
  • Since 1470: every 25 years (ordinary)
  • Extraordinary Jubilees at papal discretion (e.g., 2015 Year of Mercy)
Explain ordinary vs extraordinary jubilees and why cadence changed.
Historical Lens

The Uncomfortable Origins: An “Unarmed Crusade”

  • Indulgences via travel to Rome were not biblical in origin.
  • Conceptually adapted from Crusade ideology (rewards for journey).
  • Jubilee = spiritual crusade & risky pilgrimage.
Faggioli highlights this translation from armed to unarmed pilgrimage.
Pilgrimage Today

From Arduous Pilgrimage to Modern Tourism

  • Then: long, dangerous, deeply communal journeys.
  • Now: shorter, safer, often individualized & packaged.
  • Implication: changed spiritual dynamics.
Discuss how convenience alters the meaning and preparation of pilgrimage.
Modern Era

The 20th Century: Jubilees in a Changing World

Jubilees began to mirror global events and technologies. Three key snapshots:

  • 1950: Post-war reconciliation & Cold War framing.
  • 1966: Decentralized celebration (post–Vatican II).
  • 1975: First global TV broadcast Jubilee.
Set up next slides with each example.
1950

A Jubilee of Reconciliation — and the Cold War

  • First Jubilee after WWII: gathering divided Europe in Rome.
  • Early Cold War context: Catholicism as moral force vs. Communism.
Frame reconciliation and geopolitics shaping the Jubilee’s message.
1966 & 1975

Decentralization & Mass Media

  • 1966: Celebrating Vatican II locally — decentralized.
  • 1975: First globally televised Jubilee.
  • Adapting to new theology & technology.
Show the Church’s capacity to adapt: subsidiarity & media.
2000

The Great Jubilee: A Triumphant Church

  • Pope John Paul II, post–Communism optimism.
  • Pre-9/11 geopolitical shift; pre–abuse-crisis reckoning.
  • Confidence in a new millennium.
Capture the tone of triumph before subsequent crises.
Reckoning

Jubilee 2000: The “Purification of Memory”

  • Confession of historical failings (e.g., Crusades, anti-Semitism).
  • Major step for interfaith relations, especially Judaism.
A landmark ecclesial penitence fostering dialogue.
Now

A New Reality: The World in 2025

  • “Age of sadness”: environmental anxiety, wars, social division.
  • Widespread hopelessness & fatigue.
Contrast with 2000’s optimism; set stage for a Jubilee of Hope.
Theme

The Jubilee of 2025: A Call for Hope

Pope Francis designates a Jubilee of Hope, following the 2015 Jubilee of Mercy: from healing to hoping.

Shift in diagnosis: from lack of mercy → lack of hope.
Scripture

“Spes non confundit” — Hope Will Not Shame

  • Romans 5:5 — “Hope does not disappoint.”
  • Deeper sense: a hope you’ll never be ashamed to have.
Greek nuance suggests resilience of hope amid cynicism.
Clarification

Theological Hope ≠ Optimism

  • Optimism: a feeling things will go well.
  • Hope: a virtue — trust in God’s promise, despite odds.
  • Faggioli: hope is the opposite of mere optimism.
Anchor the keynote distinction for students.
Christology

Hope is Personified in Jesus Christ

Grounded in the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus — defeat and death are not final.

Make explicit the Christocentric foundation of Christian hope.
Orientation

Hope is Not Nostalgia

  • Not a return to a mythic “golden age.”
  • Looks forward to a different, better future — not in our control.
  • Requires letting go of the past.
Avoid romanticizing the past; cultivate openness to God’s future.
Practice

Hope as an Active Commitment

  • Not passive; a discipline to exercise.
  • An artisan craft: patiently rebuild, reconcile, repair.
  • Work even when results aren’t immediate.
Tie to Pope Francis’s emphasis on processes over events.
Conversion

Signs of Crisis → Signals of Hope

  • Work for peace; refuse nihilism with life.
  • Care for the discarded: poor, elderly, young.
  • Seek redemption for the fallen.
Make the mandate concrete and actionable.
Synodality

A Synodal Conversion: Hope is Communal

  • Pushes back against individualistic “Jesus and me.”
  • Journeying together: people physically gather in Rome.
  • Faith is embodied, shared, and dialogical.
Link Jubilee pilgrimage with synodal ecclesiology.
For Others

Hope Is for Others

Walter Benjamin: “Those for whom we are given hope are those without hope.”

  • We bear hope on behalf of others.
  • Priority for those who have lost it.
Expand the moral responsibility embedded in Christian hope.
Traditio

We Hope Because Others Hoped for Us

  • Hope is received — a gift passed through generations.
  • Ancestors, saints, communities: a chain of hope.
  • Our task: continue and hand it on.
Invite students to locate themselves within this living tradition.
Takeaway

Conclusion: The Legacy of the Jubilee of Hope

  • Reject despair; embrace demanding, active hope.
  • Work together for a future we trust but do not control.
  • Faith in action for our time.
Synthesize history, theology, and practice for today’s Church.
