Jubilee of Hope — 2025

The Catholic Jubilee: A Journey into History, Theology, and Hope

Based on a lecture by Dr. Massimo Faggioli (Villanova University)

Introduction: This presentation explores the Jubilee through the lens of Dr. Massimo Faggioli, a leading Catholic Church historian. We will trace the Jubilee's historical roots, theological meaning, and its relevance for the 2025 Jubilee of Hope.

Definitions

What is a Catholic Jubilee?

  • A holy year of grace, conversion, prayer, and repentance.
  • Inspired by the biblical jubilee (Leviticus), but distinct: focuses on spiritual renewal, pilgrimage, and indulgence.
  • Traditionally involves a pilgrimage to Rome and the possibility of gaining special indulgences.
  • Centers on a specific spiritual theme set by the Pope.
Catholic Jubilees draw inspiration from the biblical tradition of jubilee years but are unique in their focus on spiritual renewal and pilgrimage.
Origins

1300: The First Jubilee — Papacy at its Apex

Instituted by Pope Boniface VIII during the era of Christendom: close ties between church and state, strong papal authority.

The first Jubilee expressed papal leadership and the unity of Western Christendom.
Cadence

How Often Are Jubilees Held?

  • 1300: 100-year interval
  • Later: 50-year interval
  • Since 1470: every 25 years (ordinary)
  • Extraordinary Jubilees at papal discretion (e.g., 2015 Year of Mercy)
Ordinary Jubilees are regular; extraordinary Jubilees mark special occasions.
Historical Lens

Origins: Pilgrimage and Indulgence

  • Indulgences through pilgrimage to Rome, not directly biblical but rooted in medieval Christian practice.
  • Conceptually related to Crusade theology: spiritual benefit linked to journey.
  • Jubilee as a spiritual pilgrimage, not military crusade.
Jubilee transformed the idea of journey from military to spiritual.
Pilgrimage Today

From Arduous Pilgrimage to Modern Tourism

  • Then: long, dangerous, communal journeys.
  • Now: shorter, safer, often individualized and touristic.
  • Implication: changes in spiritual preparation and experience.
Modern convenience changes the nature of pilgrimage and spiritual challenge.
Modern Era

The 20th Century: Jubilees in a Changing World

Jubilees began to mirror global events and technologies. Three key snapshots:

  • 1950: Post-war reconciliation and Cold War context.
  • 1966: Decentralized celebration (post-Vatican II).
  • 1975: First global TV broadcast Jubilee.
Each Jubilee reflects the era's challenges and opportunities.
1950

A Jubilee of Reconciliation and the Cold War

  • First Jubilee after WWII: gathering divided Europe in Rome.
  • Catholicism as a moral force in a world divided by ideology.
Jubilee as a source of healing and hope in postwar Europe.
1966 & 1975

Decentralization and Mass Media

  • 1966: Celebrating Vatican II locally — decentralized.
  • 1975: First globally televised Jubilee.
  • Church adapts to new theology and technology.
Jubilee adapts to global communication and local church life.
2000

The Great Jubilee: A Triumphant Church

  • Pope John Paul II, post-Communism optimism.
  • Pre-9/11 world; hope for a new millennium.
Jubilee 2000 was marked by hope and global unity.
Reckoning

Jubilee 2000: The “Purification of Memory”

  • Confession of historical failings (Crusades, anti-Semitism, etc.).
  • Major step for interfaith relations, especially with Judaism.
The Church used the Jubilee for deep examination and healing.
Now

A New Reality: The World in 2025

  • “Age of sadness”: environmental anxiety, wars, social division.
  • Widespread hopelessness and fatigue.
The context for the Jubilee of Hope is marked by global uncertainty.
Theme

The Jubilee of 2025: A Call for Hope

Pope Francis proclaims a Jubilee of Hope, moving from healing (2015 Jubilee of Mercy) to hoping.

The Church responds to a world in need of hope, not just mercy.
Scripture

“Spes non confundit” — Hope Will Not Shame

  • Romans 5:5 — “Hope does not disappoint, because God's love has been poured into our hearts through the Holy Spirit.”
  • Christian hope is rooted in God’s faithfulness, not mere optimism.
Hope is a theological virtue, sustained by grace.
Clarification

Theological Hope ≠ Optimism

  • Optimism: a feeling that things will go well.
  • Hope: a virtue — trust in God’s promise, sustained by grace even in adversity.
  • Christian hope is anchored in Christ, not circumstances.
Distinguish between secular optimism and theological hope.
Christology

Hope is Personified in Jesus Christ

Christian hope is grounded in the resurrection: defeat and death are not final, because Christ is risen.

Hope has a face: Jesus Christ.
Orientation

Hope is Not Nostalgia

  • Not a longing for a mythic “golden age.”
  • Looks forward to a future shaped by God’s promise.
  • Requires letting go of the past and openness to God’s future.
Christian hope is future-oriented, not romanticizing the past.
Practice

Hope as an Active Commitment

  • Hope is not passive; it is a discipline practiced daily.
  • An artisan craft: patiently rebuilding, reconciling, repairing.
  • Cooperation with grace, even when results are not immediate.
Pope Francis emphasizes processes over events: hope is a habit, not a one-time act.
Conversion

Signs of Crisis → Signals of Hope

  • Work for peace; choose life over nihilism.
  • Care for those on the margins: poor, elderly, young.
  • Seek redemption and reconciliation.
Christian hope is concrete and actionable.
Synodality

Hope is Communal: Synodal Conversion

  • Not individualistic: faith is lived together, not alone.
  • Pilgrimage to Rome symbolizes journeying as Church.
  • Synodality: discernment, listening, and walking together as a people.
The Jubilee is a communal event, embodying the Church's synodal character.
For Others

Hope Is for Others

Walter Benjamin: “Those for whom we are given hope are those without hope.”

  • We bear hope on behalf of others, especially those who have lost it.
  • Christian hope is always directed outward: for the suffering, the marginalized, the lost.
Hope becomes real when shared with those most in need.
Traditio

We Hope Because Others Hoped for Us

  • Hope is received — a gift passed through generations, saints, and the Church.
  • We are part of a chain of hope, sustained by the communion of saints.
  • Our calling: receive, live, and hand on hope to others.
Locate yourself within the living tradition of Christian hope.
Takeaway

Conclusion: The Legacy of the Jubilee of Hope

  • Reject despair; embrace demanding, active hope.
  • Work together for a future we trust but do not control.
  • Live faith in action for our time.
History, theology, and practice converge in the Jubilee’s call to hope.
